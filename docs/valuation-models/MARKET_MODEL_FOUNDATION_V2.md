# MARKET_MODEL_FOUNDATION_V2.md

**Document status:** Governing constitutional foundation. Supersedes and replaces `MARKET_MODEL_FOUNDATION.md` and `MARKET_MODEL_FOUNDATION_AMENDMENT.md`, which are historical artifacts with no remaining authority.
**Purpose:** Permanent conceptual and governance specification for all fantasy football valuation models built on this platform.
**Scope:** Quarterbacks, running backs, wide receivers, tight ends, rookie prospects, current and future rookie picks, and multi-asset packages, across weekly, rest-of-season, one-year, three-year, and dynasty horizons.
**Exclusion:** This document defines no production formula, fitted coefficient, model weight, vendor selection, or player price.

# PART I — CONSTITUTIONAL AUTHORITY

## 1. Purpose

The platform is a price-discovery and decision-support system, not a rankings page. Its defining capability is the disciplined separation of what the market currently charges for an asset from what the evidence says the asset is worth, with explicit uncertainty attached to both.

Every model, feature, score, document, and interface element built on this foundation exists to estimate exactly one of the canonical valuation objects defined in Section 4, or to govern, explain, or validate such an estimate. No component may blur those objects for convenience.

The product succeeds when users make better decisions under uncertainty. It fails when it manufactures false precision, false signals, or false confidence — regardless of engagement metrics.

## 2. Interpretation Rule

Every future document that introduces a model, feature, score, or transformation must answer all ten questions below. A specification that omits or contradicts any answer is non-compliant and may not enter production.

1. What mathematical object is being estimated (the estimand)?
2. In what native unit does it exist before display transformation?
3. What information is permitted to enter it?
4. What information is forbidden from entering it?
5. What league environment and time horizon condition it?
6. What uncertainty object accompanies it?
7. Which downstream outputs may consume it?
8. How is it validated, and against what baselines?
9. How is it reproduced historically at point-in-time fidelity?
10. How is it explained to users?

## 3. Prohibited Unqualified Terms

The following terms are prohibited in technical specifications unless immediately qualified with the canonical object intended:

- "player value"
- "dynasty value"
- "true value"
- "fair price"
- "consensus value"
- "risk"
- "confidence"
- "volatility"

"Predict value" is not an estimand. "Risk" without a named risk object is not a specification.

# PART II — VALUE ONTOLOGY

## 4. Canonical Valuation Objects

The platform recognizes exactly six valuation objects. They are related but never interchangeable. Every valuation output in the system is one of these objects or a governed transformation of one.

The canonical pipeline order is:

> Expected Fantasy Output → Fundamental Player Utility → Intrinsic Asset Value ⟷ Observed Market Price → User-Specific Decision Value → Executable Trade Estimate

Intrinsic Asset Value and Observed Market Price are estimated independently and compared; neither feeds the other.

### 4.1 Expected Fantasy Output

**Definition:** A probability distribution over future fantasy scoring outcomes for a declared player, position, league scoring system, and time horizon.

**Purpose:** The forecast layer. It answers "what might this player score?" and nothing else.

**Permitted inputs:** expected games played; weekly scoring distributions; role-state probabilities; opportunity distributions; efficiency distributions; team and schedule context; injury and availability states.

**Forbidden inputs:** current fantasy-market demand; expert rankings used as a price signal; current acquisition cost; current trade liquidity; user roster needs; current market sentiment (except inside a separately named market-informed research forecast — see Section 12.8).

**Dependencies:** consumes player-state estimation; feeds Fundamental Player Utility only.

Expected Fantasy Output is a forecast. It is not a price and is not sufficient by itself to determine asset value.

### 4.2 Fundamental Player Utility

**Definition:** The model-estimated fantasy benefit generated by owning and deploying the player within a declared league environment and time horizon, before current market demand or acquisition cost is considered.

**Purpose:** Converts a scoring forecast into league-conditioned usefulness. It answers "what is that production worth under these league rules?"

**Permitted inputs:** Expected Fantasy Output; replacement-level alternatives; positional and lineup scarcity; probability of being startable; marginal lineup advantage; expected usable games and seasons; role continuity; injury and availability risk; conditional upside and downside; option value arising from football states; roster-slot consumption at the reference-league level; discounting of future utility.

**Forbidden inputs:** current Observed Market Price; current expert consensus as a valuation input; current social popularity; current trade demand; current market liquidity premiums; current market preferences for age, name, hype, or aesthetics; the user's specific roster state; the assets required to acquire the player.

**Dependencies:** consumes Expected Fantasy Output plus the replacement/scarcity environment; feeds Intrinsic Asset Value only.

### 4.3 Intrinsic Asset Value

**Definition:** Fundamental Player Utility translated into the common latent valuation unit (Section 5), plus only those non-price asset characteristics that have a defined independent utility mechanism.

**Purpose:** The platform's evidence-based estimate of asset worth, expressed in the unit where market comparison occurs.

**Permitted additional inputs beyond Fundamental Player Utility:** football-derived option value; expected control duration; future roster eligibility; explicit flexibility benefits that exist independently of present market popularity; future-pick or contract rights where the modeled asset contains such rights; nonlinear value created by scarce elite lineup advantage.

**Forbidden inputs:** current Market Price; current consensus ranking; current social sentiment; current trade frequency used as a popularity premium; current willingness to pay merely because users prefer the player; market-source momentum; current resale demand.

**Dependencies:** consumes Fundamental Player Utility and the latent mapping; feeds classification, package reference valuation, and the Decision Layer.

**Binding independence rule:** The production mispricing system must maintain a **Pure Intrinsic Asset Value** that excludes current fantasy-market price and current fantasy-market preference signals. A separately named **Market-Informed Forecast** or **Resale-Adjusted Asset Estimate** may be researched, but it must expose its market dependencies, must never replace Pure Intrinsic Asset Value, and must never be the sole basis of a mispricing classification.

### 4.4 Observed Market Price

**Definition:** The posterior estimate of the current exchange cost required to acquire an asset in a declared fantasy environment, based solely on market observations and their reliability, expressed in the common latent valuation unit.

**Purpose:** The independent estimate of what the market currently charges. Mispricing cannot be measured without it.

**Permitted inputs:** completed trades; draft selections; auction prices; waiver bids; expert and crowd rankings treated as stated beliefs; platform behavior; roster and exposure behavior; trade-calculator outputs treated as derived opinions; current sentiment and attention where they explain demand; source liquidity, dispersion, recency, and reliability.

**Forbidden inputs:** the platform's Pure Intrinsic Asset Value; the platform's opinion that the player should be worth more or less; future fantasy output except as revealed through observed external market behavior; user-specific roster utility.

**Dependencies:** consumes classified market observations (Section 9); feeds classification, the Executable Trade Estimate, and the Decision Layer.

Observed Market Price is an estimate of consensus acquisition cost with an uncertainty range and a confidence assessment. It is not a guaranteed executable quote.

### 4.5 User-Specific Decision Value

**Definition:** The marginal utility of adding or removing an asset for a particular user's roster, league, competitive window, alternatives, constraints, and risk preferences.

**Purpose:** Translates global reference values into a recommendation relevant to one roster.

**Permitted inputs:** Intrinsic Asset Value; Observed Market Price; the user's starting lineup, bench, and taxi composition; contender or rebuilding state; replacement availability; positional concentration; correlated roster exposures; playoff goals; declared risk tolerance; roster-slot constraints; opportunity cost; expected future transaction needs.

**Forbidden behaviors:** claims of universal worth; hidden modification of the canonical Intrinsic Asset Value or Observed Market Price; unsupported assumptions about the user's strategy.

**Dependencies:** strictly downstream of both reference values. A user-specific adjustment must be displayed as an adjustment to the reference value, never silently merged into it.

### 4.6 Executable Trade Estimate

**Definition:** The estimated transaction range and probability that a specific acquisition or disposal can be completed under current market conditions.

**Purpose:** Distinguishes what an asset costs in consensus terms from what a real transaction is likely to require.

**Permitted inputs:** Observed Market Price; bid–ask uncertainty; trade-package structure; league-specific scarcity; counterparty need; market liquidity; consolidation premiums; package discounts; roster-slot costs; time to transact; historical execution rates where available.

**Forbidden behavior:** presentation as an exact or guaranteed price.

**Dependencies:** downstream of Observed Market Price and package valuation.

## 5. Common Latent Valuation Unit

### 5.1 Requirement

Intrinsic Asset Value and Observed Market Price must exist in a single **common latent valuation unit** before either is transformed into any display scale. The latent unit — not the display scale — is the canonical comparison space for valuation gaps, fair-value bands, mispricing classification, package aggregation, pick equivalence, trade analysis, and historical error measurement.

### 5.2 Required Properties

The common latent unit must be:

- monotonic in modeled fantasy asset utility;
- conditioned on league format and horizon;
- capable of representing replacement, starter, and elite separation;
- capable of representing negative or near-zero marginal utility internally;
- aggregable only under declared package rules (Section 15);
- stable enough for historical comparison;
- invertible to the display scale within the supported player universe;
- accompanied by an uncertainty object.

### 5.3 Acceptable Implementations

This document does not mandate one implementation. Acceptable candidates include:

- expected discounted marginal lineup utility;
- expected contribution to season-win probability above replacement;
- a latent exchange-capital unit inferred jointly from market observations and anchored to utility;
- another empirically validated utility numeraire.

The selected implementation must beat simpler alternatives out of sample and satisfy the governance requirements of this document before it is frozen.

### 5.4 Prohibited Implementation

The platform must never:

1. independently map raw intrinsic estimates and raw market estimates to separate 0–100 distributions;
2. subtract the resulting displayed scores; and
3. label that difference as mispricing.

Independent percentile or quantile mappings can manufacture, enlarge, shrink, or reverse apparent disagreement. A displayed-score difference is descriptive only; it is never the statistical basis of classification.

### 5.5 Comparison Procedure

The required order of operations is:

1. Estimate the intrinsic object in its native model space.
2. Convert it into the common latent valuation unit.
3. Estimate Observed Market Price directly in the same latent unit.
4. Estimate uncertainty and dependence for both objects, including covariance where they share any data or assumptions.
5. Calculate the valuation gap and classification in latent space.
6. Transform each object into the display scale for presentation using the shared mapping (Section 20.2).
7. Display the classification derived from latent space.

### 5.6 Valuation Gap

The canonical gap is:

> Intrinsic Asset Value minus Observed Market Price, measured in the common latent valuation unit.

Classification of the gap must additionally consider intrinsic uncertainty, market-price uncertainty, shared-data covariance, liquidity, transaction range, horizon alignment, format alignment, unresolved event risk, and model-version compatibility (Section 21).

# PART III — BINDING PRINCIPLES

## 6. Core Principles

The principles below are binding design constraints. Future modeling decisions must comply or explicitly document why an exception is justified. Where a principle is elaborated by a later section, the later section controls the details.

**P1 — Separate price from value.** Market consensus and model-estimated utility are different objects with separate pipelines, separate uncertainty, and separate explanations. Combining them destroys the ability to detect disagreement.

**P2 — No context-free value.** Fantasy value is conditional on league rules, lineup requirements, scoring, roster depth, and horizon. A canonical reference format is permitted as a quotation convention only; it must never be described as universal.

**P3 — Horizon is part of the asset definition.** Switching horizon changes the underlying valuation logic, not the label on the same score.

**P4 — Transparency beats unnecessary complexity.** Complexity is justified only by measurable out-of-sample improvement or materially better uncertainty estimates. A model that cannot be audited may not be the permanent intellectual core of the product.

**P5 — Stability beats noise; responsiveness must be proportional to structural change.** Outcomes are decomposed into repeatable and non-repeatable components. Update size reflects information content, not emotional salience. Confirmed structural events may bypass normal smoothing; ambiguous events may not.

**P6 — Opportunity usually stabilizes before fantasy outcomes.** Role signals (snaps, routes, targets, carries, designed touches) generally carry more forward-looking information than isolated scoring results, subject to position-specific validation.

**P7 — Efficiency must be regressed.** Observed efficiency requires sample-aware shrinkage toward appropriate position, role, and player priors.

**P8 — Small samples deserve skepticism, not dismissal.** Update by sample size and signal quality jointly; do not wait for arbitrary thresholds before recognizing genuine role evidence.

**P9 — Uncertainty is modeled, not hidden.** Internally, valuations are distributions. Point estimates are summaries of the belief state, not the belief state itself.

**P10 — Confidence and value are orthogonal.** Low confidence must never be silently converted into a generic value penalty. Uncertainty may affect value only through the named mechanisms in Section 17.8.

**P11 — Volatility is not uncertainty.** Uncertainty is lack of knowledge about an estimate; volatility is expected movement in a named object. They have different definitions, features, and displays (Section 18).

**P12 — Every material movement has a decomposed reason.** The pipeline retains driver-level attribution and event provenance, and separates model, market, and normalization effects (Section 22.3).

**P13 — Correlation is not independent evidence.** Overlapping signals must be grouped into conceptual families, tested for redundancy, and combined or regularized. Ten derivative observations of one underlying source are one observation.

**P14 — Interactions are core, not decorative.** The architecture supports conditional effects from the start, under the governance of Section 12.

**P15 — Replacement is a league property; scarcity is usable marginal advantage.** Replacement baselines are computed by format. Scarcity is derived from projected marginal lineup advantage and availability, never from position labels.

**P16 — Age is a probability modifier, not a scheduled cliff.** Age effects are position-specific, conditional, and explicit about survivorship bias.

**P17 — Injury is a state, a history, and an information problem.** Injury modeling separates confirmed status, estimated severity, recurrence, role consequences, and information confidence.

**P18 — Contracts matter through incentives and opportunity.** Contract signals affect opportunity durability and transition risk; they never add generic value directly.

**P19 — News is classified evidence, not a score multiplier.** News is graded by source quality, event type, confirmation, novelty, and expected structural impact.

**P20 — Sentiment is market information.** Attention and enthusiasm inform Observed Market Price, momentum, and overreaction detection. They enter intrinsic pipelines only when validated as incremental football evidence and only through the event pathway in Section 24.3.

**P21 — Market movement is not automatically information.** Market Price updates require source breadth, effective sample size, recency, and reliability controls.

**P22 — "Already priced in" never suppresses an intrinsic update.** Market incorporation may affect Market Price, momentum, classification, and actionability. If information changes the estimated football state or future utility, the intrinsic estimate updates regardless of what the market has done.

**P23 — Missing data reduce confidence before they distort value.** The system distinguishes unavailable, delayed, structurally missing, not applicable, and true zero. Silent zero imputation is prohibited unless zero is the verified semantic value.

**P24 — Model disagreement is a feature.** Components may disagree for valid reasons; the platform exposes the disagreement and identifies the evidence that would resolve it, rather than fabricating narrative unity.

**P25 — Backtests reproduce the information available at the time.** Timestamped data, versioned features, and season-replay infrastructure are mandatory.

**P26 — Calibration matters more than isolated hits.** A model that says "80 percent" must earn that language across comparable cases.

**P27 — The product must know what it cannot know.** Partially observable variables receive stated uncertainty and competing explanations, never fabricated precision.

**P28 — Version everything.** Models, features, data, normalization, and explanations all carry versions, and historical outputs remain attached to the versions that produced them.

# PART IV — ENVIRONMENT, DATA, AND MARKET OBSERVATION

## 7. League, Asset, and Identity Schema

### 7.1 League Schema

Every valuation is conditioned on a versioned league schema containing, where relevant: number of teams; scoring rules; starting lineup slots; flex and superflex eligibility; tight-end premium; bench size; injured reserve; taxi squad; keeper rules; salary or contract rules; waiver environment; playoff structure; season phase; replacement assumptions.

A named preset (e.g., "12-team superflex TEP") is an alias for a full schema, not a substitute for one.

### 7.2 Asset Schema

The system supports explicit asset types, each with its own uncertainty and settlement rules:

- active NFL player;
- rookie prospect;
- current rookie pick;
- future rookie pick;
- conditional pick;
- contract or salary asset where supported;
- roster slot or waiver claim where supported.

Rookie picks and packages are first-class assets governed by Section 15.

### 7.3 Identity Governance

Canonical identity must handle: provider-specific IDs; duplicate names; name changes; team changes; position changes; pre-draft rookies; unsigned players; retired players; aliases; merged or split provider records.

Historical values remain attached to the correct canonical entity after transactions or metadata changes.

## 8. Data Readiness Tiers and Contracts

### 8.1 Production Tiers

Every proposed input belongs to exactly one tier.

**Tier 1 — Launch-Critical Guaranteed Data.** Required for the minimum credible product and obtainable through reliable, legal, affordable, historically testable sources. The product must remain functional when only Tier 1 data are available. Tier 1 candidates include: canonical player identity; games; snaps; routes; targets; carries; core box-score production; age; depth chart; injury status; team; market observations; league format.

**Tier 2 — Licensed Production Enhancement.** Expected to improve performance materially but dependent on a paid or restricted provider. Requires confirmed commercial rights, cost approval, historical access, service-level expectations, fallback behavior, and a vendor-exit plan.

**Tier 3 — Experimental Research Data.** Promising but insufficiently validated, sparse, expensive, unstable, or operationally difficult. Tier 3 signals may not influence production values until promoted through governance.

**Tier 4 — Rejected or Unavailable.** Legally inaccessible, operationally fragile, economically unjustified, irreproducible, or empirically unsupported. Tier 4 concepts must not appear in production architecture as assumed dependencies.

A concept mentioned anywhere in this document is not thereby an approved production dependency. Tier assignment and the legal gate control.

### 8.2 Mandatory Data Contract

Every production signal requires: canonical definition; unit; provider; legal basis or license; first available season; coverage; point-in-time availability; refresh cadence; expected latency; correction policy; missingness behavior; provider-specific quirks; identity-matching policy; estimated annual cost; backup source or degradation path; owner; monitoring rule.

### 8.3 Vendor-Neutral Features

Core football concepts are defined independently of any provider's column names. Provider adapters map vendor fields into canonical concepts; vendor definitions must not silently redefine the model.

### 8.4 Data Failure Behavior

Every critical signal declares its behavior when data are late, unavailable, contradictory, revised, structurally missing, or outside known ranges. Permitted responses: validated fallback; increased uncertainty; stale-value warning; restricted classification; temporary No Classification; rejection of the valuation. Silent zero imputation is prohibited unless zero is the verified semantic value.

### 8.5 Legal Release Gate

No signal enters production solely because it can be scraped technically. Production approval requires documented rights, permitted usage, or another defensible legal basis reviewed by the responsible owner.

## 9. Market Observation and Sources

### 9.1 Source Classes

Every market input is classified as exactly one of:

1. Executed Transaction
2. Draft or Auction Behavior
3. Platform Behavioral Observation
4. Stated Expert Belief
5. Stated Crowd Belief
6. Derived Consensus Product
7. Attention or Sentiment Signal
8. Synthetic or Model-Generated Observation

These classes are not equally direct evidence of acquisition cost, and the Market Price model must not treat them as such.

### 9.2 Source Lineage

Every source retains: original publisher or platform; observation type; timestamp; league format; horizon; asset package; upstream dependencies where known; update methodology where known; version; rights and licensing status.

### 9.3 Independence and Effective Sample Size

Nominal observation count is not effective sample size. The Market Price model must adjust for: copied rankings; shared upstream data; expert herding; duplicate reposting; one transaction appearing on multiple sites; calculators derived from the same consensus source; platform-user overlap.

### 9.4 Transactions and Selection Bias

Completed trades are selected observations, not random samples of consensus value: only disagreements transact; rejected offers are unseen; contender and rebuilder motives differ; league culture, distress, and manager skill vary; transaction data overrepresent active leagues. Executed trades may be high-quality evidence without being unbiased evidence, and the Market Price model must account for the selection mechanism.

### 9.5 Package Decomposition

A multi-asset trade must not be decomposed into player prices through arbitrary equal splitting or circular use of the target valuation system. Acceptable approaches include probabilistic package models, jointly estimated latent prices, constrained inference from repeated trade networks, identifiable single-asset anchors, or explicit exclusion when decomposition uncertainty is too high.

### 9.6 Market Range and Execution Range

Observed Market Price is always accompanied by a range reflecting observation uncertainty. Where possible the system distinguishes: consensus estimation range; expected executable acquisition range; expected executable sale range; estimated spread.

### 9.7 Reflexivity

If the platform becomes influential, its published values may affect the market it measures. The system monitors downstream copying, source contamination, post-publication movement, declining source independence, apparent accuracy caused by market adoption, and signal decay caused by users acting on recommendations. The platform must not claim discovery when it may be measuring its own influence.

# PART V — VALUATION ARCHITECTURE

## 10. Canonical Dependency Order

The required dependency order is:

1. Identity and league schema
2. Point-in-time data layer
3. Player-state estimation
4. Position-specific forecast distributions (Expected Fantasy Output)
5. Replacement and scarcity environment
6. Fundamental Player Utility
7. Common latent valuation mapping
8. Intrinsic Asset Value
9. Independent Observed Market Price estimation
10. Confidence and uncertainty objects
11. Volatility and event-sensitivity objects
12. Mispricing classification
13. Package valuation
14. User-Specific Decision Value
15. Display normalization and explanation

A downstream output must never silently feed an upstream object. No single input may jump directly to a final value without passing through the appropriate layer.

### 10.1 Dependency Matrix

| Object | Required upstream inputs | Forbidden direct inputs | Primary downstream consumers |
|---|---|---|---|
| Expected Fantasy Output | player state, opportunity, efficiency, team, health, schedule | current Market Price, user roster need | Fundamental Player Utility |

## 11. Layer Specifications

Each layer produces an estimate plus metadata covering uncertainty, freshness, provenance, and key drivers. The layers below define player-state estimation and its supporting evidence systems; the valuation objects they feed are defined in Section 4.

### 11.1 Prior State

Establishes the belief state before the newest evidence: prior valuation and distribution, historical performance, age and experience, draft capital and prospect profile, prior role, health history, contract and roster status, position-specific career patterns. Strong priors supported by large samples update slowly; weak priors update quickly. Prior version and timestamp are retained.

### 11.2 Production

Describes realized output (fantasy points; passing, rushing, receiving production; touchdowns; explosive plays; first downs; red-zone outcomes; game context) and decomposes it into opportunity-driven and efficiency-driven components with persistence estimates and data-quality flags. Production without usage support moves value less. Fantasy points are an outcome to explain, not a sufficient forecast.

### 11.3 Opportunity

Estimates the quantity and quality of chances available to the player (snaps, routes, dropbacks, attempts, targets, carries, designed touches, goal-line work, situational participation, air yards, end-zone and red-zone opportunities, team play volume) and outputs role size, role quality, high-value opportunity, role trend, role durability, competition share, and future-usage uncertainty. Opportunity is conditional on health, game script, coaching, personnel, and team strength.

### 11.4 Efficiency

Estimates how effectively the player converts opportunity into output, using position-specific rate statistics, expected-points and over-expectation models, and licensed tracking or charting features where validated. Outputs estimated conversion skill with uncertainty, context adjustment, regression targets, and sustainability assessment. Efficiency must be conditioned on opportunity type, teammates, opponent, scheme, and game state, and separated into stable skill and unstable result components.

### 11.5 Team Context

Estimates the environment that creates or suppresses opportunity and efficiency: offensive quality, scoring environment, pace, pass rate over expectation, offensive line, quarterback quality, coaching, depth chart, target and carry competition, game-script tendencies, and horizon-appropriate schedule. Outputs the team opportunity pool, scoring opportunity, role competition, environment stability, and transition risk.

### 11.6 Age

Modifies expected development, decline, career length, and uncertainty using chronological age, experience, position, workload history, breakout timing, draft profile, performance level, injury history, role type, and comparable cohorts. Outputs development and decline probabilities, expected remaining usable seasons, age-related variance, and horizon-specific adjustment. Age effects grow with horizon and have almost no direct weekly effect except through current physical state.

### 11.7 Injury

Estimates availability, performance limitation, recurrence, and role disruption from official designations, practice participation, reported diagnosis, procedure and recovery information, injury history, positional movement demands, and source reliability. Outputs probability of playing, expected workload if active, performance limitation, reinjury risk, recovery distribution, long-term impairment risk, and information confidence. Injury is never a binary active/inactive flag.

### 11.8 Contract

Estimates team commitment, roster security, transition risk, and role continuity from contract structure, guarantees, cap consequences, release and trade flexibility, and team investment. Contract signals affect opportunity durability and transition risk (P18); they never add generic value.

### 11.9 News

Converts unstructured events into classified evidence with event type, source reliability, confirmation level, novelty, affected layers, expected duration, estimated structural importance, and unresolved questions. Confirmed transactions may update role immediately; ambiguous commentary may only raise uncertainty; unconfirmed news effects decay if unsupported (Section 19.4).

### 11.10 Sentiment

Measures public attention, conviction, and narrative pressure (expert rank changes, discussion volume, search interest, roster and draft behavior, engagement, polarity, dispersion). Sentiment informs Observed Market Price, momentum, and overreaction detection. It is source- and manipulation-aware, and it enters intrinsic pipelines only through the event pathway of Section 24.3.

### 11.11 Momentum

Describes the direction, speed, breadth, and persistence of change in a named value object over a specified window. Momentum is reported separately for Observed Market Price and Intrinsic Asset Value, never feeds Intrinsic Asset Value, and may signal stale models or overreaction. Momentum describes trend, not level.

## 12. Signal Combination Rules

### 12.1 Additive Failure

Purely additive models fail when the value of one signal depends on another (volume and efficiency; age and position; target share and route participation; quarterback quality and target value; injury and role competition; sentiment and liquidity) and can produce absurd compensation, where a severe weakness in a necessary component is canceled by unrelated strengths.

### 12.2 Interaction Criteria

An interaction may be introduced only when at least one holds: one variable changes the interpretation of another; two variables jointly create an outcome neither captures alone; a necessary condition exists; the combined effect is materially nonlinear; domain logic predicts the interaction before fitting; out-of-sample testing confirms incremental value; the interaction improves calibration or uncertainty estimation. In-sample search improvement alone is insufficient.

### 12.3 Necessary-Condition Gates

Some signals function as probabilistic gates: a receiver cannot realize target value without routes; a quarterback cannot produce without an expected start; contingent upside requires a plausible path to workload; a suspended player has near-zero weekly availability; dynasty value requires some probability of future roster and role access. Gates are probabilistic unless the underlying state is genuinely binary and confirmed.

### 12.4 Saturation and Thresholds

Signals may exhibit diminishing marginal effects, and the model must permit saturation rather than linear extrapolation. Real cliffs exist (starting job, full-time route role, goal-line responsibility, starter thresholds, replacement lines) but thresholds must be validated and usually smoothed; hard cliffs create instability around arbitrary cutoffs.

### 12.5 Interaction Governance

Every interaction carries: a football rationale; position scope; horizon scope; data-availability statement; uncertainty treatment; ablation test; stability test; explanation template; failure-mode review.

### 12.6 Weight Discipline

A weight has no meaning outside a declared target, position, horizon, feature definition, model family, training window, league format, and validation metric. Statements such as "opportunity should be 40 percent of value" are rejected without full estimation context.

A signal deserves greater influence when it has strong incremental out-of-sample predictive power, reliable measurement, decision-time availability, cross-season stability, a football mechanism, calibration benefit, non-redundancy, cohort robustness, and explainability. Influence is capped — through shrinkage, saturation, robust losses, priors, or scenario constraints — when the signal is volatile, sparse, manipulable, unreliable at extremes, saturating, regime-dependent, methodologically opaque, capable of implausible outputs, or double-counted.

### 12.7 Priors, Shrinkage, and Dynamics

Estimation favors disciplined shrinkage and hierarchical priors (league → position → archetype → player), because NFL samples are small, careers are short, roles change, and candidate features are numerous. Weights may vary by era, regime, scoring environment, career stage, sample size, format, and horizon, but dynamic weighting is constrained: a model whose weights swing dramatically week to week is fitting noise.

### 12.8 Market-Informed Research Models

Market data may improve forecasts because crowds aggregate information, but including market price inside the intrinsic pipeline erases the disagreement the product exists to detect. Policy: maintain the Pure Intrinsic Asset Value excluding current market price; optionally maintain a separately named market-informed forecast ensemble as a research output; never use the market-informed output as the sole basis of classification; report when market information improves accuracy at the cost of independence.

## 13. Time Horizons

### 13.1 Required Horizons

The system maintains separate valuation states for **Weekly**, **Rest of Season**, **One Year**, **Three Years**, and **Dynasty**. These are different utility functions and uncertainty structures, not date filters.

- **Weekly:** availability, projected role, matchup, team totals, game environment, game script, lineup rules. Age, contract, and career value have almost no direct influence.
- **Rest of Season:** role durability, health, remaining schedule, expected games, competition, replacement value across remaining weeks.
- **One Year:** current- and next-season production, contract transition, depth-chart change, incoming rookies, free agency, coaching continuity, near-term development or decline.
- **Three Years:** expected usable seasons, development and decline, role survival, contract transitions, positional career shape, discounting of distant production.
- **Dynasty:** governed by Section 14.

### 13.2 Horizon UX

The interface presents one primary value with selectable horizons, defaulted by the user's league profile, with a compact strip for the others. Five simultaneous headline numbers and one universal number are both prohibited.

### 13.3 Cross-Horizon Consistency

Values need not be monotonic across horizons, but major inconsistencies must be explainable. The system flags strong weekly value with weak dynasty value, weak current value with strong long-term value, unusually large horizon gaps, and horizon divergence driven by injury, age, contract, or role transition.

## 14. Dynasty Constitution

### 14.1 Prohibited Single Definition

"Dynasty value" may never represent both long-term utility and current exchange demand. The platform maintains four separate dynasty outputs.

### 14.2 Dynasty Fundamental Utility

The discounted distribution of future fantasy utility generated under ongoing ownership, subject to explicit forecast limits: near- and medium-term production, expected usable seasons, development and decline, role survival, injury and replacement states, future team transitions, football-derived option value, uncertainty and asymmetry, discounting. It excludes current market preference and current acquisition cost.

### 14.3 Dynasty Intrinsic Asset Value

Dynasty Fundamental Utility converted into the common latent valuation unit, including only permitted non-price asset characteristics. Flexibility or future control may be recognized only when independently defined and not a proxy for present popularity.

### 14.4 Dynasty Market Price

Current estimated exchange cost in the declared dynasty format. It may legitimately reflect age preferences, contender and rebuilder demand, liquidity, rookie-pick alternatives, narratives, resale expectations, and current trade behavior — even where the intrinsic model considers those signals irrational. Market irrationality is a property of the price, not a license to contaminate the intrinsic estimate.

### 14.5 Dynasty Decision Value

Roster-specific marginal utility for a particular dynasty manager. A contender, a rebuilder, and the neutral reference roster may assign different Decision Values while sharing the same reference Intrinsic Asset Value and Market Price.

### 14.6 Forecast Horizon Discipline

Dynasty valuation must not imply precise career forecasting. Required policy: model explicit seasons only while forecast validity is defensible; assign declining weight to distant periods; use terminal-state or continuation-value methods beyond the explicit window; report sensitivity to discount rates and terminal assumptions; widen uncertainty with horizon; never claim dynasty value equals projected career points.

## 15. Rookie Picks and Packages

### 15.1 Picks Are First-Class Assets

Rookie picks are probabilistic assets with their own uncertainty and settlement rules, not labels appended to player rankings.

### 15.2 Current Rookie Pick Value

Current-pick Intrinsic Asset Value reflects: the expected prospect distribution available at the pick; class strength and depth; positional composition; probability distribution over pick outcomes; league format; roster-slot and taxi implications; option value from delaying selection; information expected before the draft. Pick liquidity and hype belong to the pick's Market Price, not its intrinsic estimate.

### 15.3 Future Rookie Pick Value

Future picks additionally require: a distribution over the eventual slot; time discounting; class-strength uncertainty; originating-team strength where available; the value of waiting for information; explicit terminal assumptions. A future first-round pick is a distribution, not a fixed asset.

### 15.4 Package Nonlinearity

The value of a package is not generally the arithmetic sum of displayed scores. Package models must consider: roster-slot consumption; consolidation premium; elite scarcity; replacement access; package liquidity; conditional assets; correlated player outcomes; diminishing utility of redundant depth. Package aggregation occurs in the common latent unit under declared rules (Section 5.2).

### 15.5 Package Value Objects

The platform distinguishes: **Reference Package Value** (canonical league, neutral roster); **User Decision Package Value** (the user's actual roster and strategy); **Market Package Price** (observed acquisition cost for the package or inferred components under Section 9.5).

## 16. Roster-Specific Decision Layer

### 16.1 Separation Rule

Roster personalization occurs strictly downstream of canonical Intrinsic Asset Value and Observed Market Price and never rewrites the global reference values silently.

### 16.2 Inputs and Outputs

Inputs: roster composition, starting lineup, bench depth, record and competitive window, replacement options, positional concentration, teammate and offense exposure, future picks, expected transaction opportunities, declared risk preference, playoff objectives, roster-slot constraints.

Outputs: user-adjusted acquisition and sale values, expected roster and lineup improvement, depth and concentration effects, contender/rebuilder fit, opportunity cost, acceptable acquisition and sale ranges, and the reason for divergence from the reference value.

### 16.3 No Universal Buy or Sell Command

The platform never issues a universal "buy" or "sell" solely from the global valuation gap. Reference classifications are limited to: **Market Below Model Range**; **Market Inside Model Range**; **Market Above Model Range**; **No Classification**. User-specific actions may be offered only after roster and strategy context are applied.

# PART VI — UNCERTAINTY, MOVEMENT, AND DISPLAY

## 17. Confidence Constitution

### 17.1 No Single Internal Confidence Object

The platform maintains six separate uncertainty and confidence objects internally. A composite user-facing score may be created only after these objects are separately modeled and validated.

1. State Uncertainty
2. Forecast Uncertainty
3. Intrinsic-Estimate Confidence
4. Market-Price Confidence
5. Data-Quality Confidence
6. Explanation Confidence

### 17.2 State Uncertainty

Uncertainty about the player's current football state: starting-role probability, injury severity, role share, current skill level, depth-chart standing.

### 17.3 Forecast Uncertainty

Dispersion in future outcomes after conditioning on the current information state, including normal football randomness and future state transitions.

### 17.4 Intrinsic-Estimate Confidence

The degree of empirical support for the estimated Intrinsic Asset Value and its interval coverage, informed by data adequacy, model fit for comparable cases, calibration, sensitivity, missingness, regime stability, state uncertainty, and model disagreement. It is not the probability that the player will succeed.

### 17.5 Market-Price Confidence

The degree to which the estimated Market Price is supported by recent, independent, relevant, and sufficiently liquid observations: source breadth, effective observation count, source independence, recency, format specificity, transaction-versus-opinion mix, dispersion, package-decomposition uncertainty, provider reliability.

### 17.6 Data-Quality Confidence

Confidence that required inputs are accurate, complete, timely, correctly mapped, and legally usable, distinguishing unavailable, delayed, structurally missing, not applicable, provider disagreement, and true zero.

### 17.7 Explanation Confidence

Confidence that the displayed explanation identifies the principal model-attributed contributors to an output or movement. Explanation Confidence never establishes causality.

### 17.8 Confidence and Value Rule

Low confidence must not automatically become a generic value penalty. Uncertainty may influence value only when: the utility function values upside or penalizes downside; future roster usability is state-dependent; option value is real; replacement consequences are asymmetric; or a declared user risk preference is applied in the Decision Layer. The model must state which mechanism is operating.

### 17.9 Epistemic and Aleatoric Separation

The platform distinguishes **epistemic uncertainty** (incomplete knowledge, limited data, model uncertainty, ambiguous state) from **aleatoric uncertainty** (irreducible or scenario-driven outcome variability). Epistemic uncertainty primarily affects confidence and interval width. Aleatoric outcome distributions may affect Fundamental Player Utility when the utility function is nonlinear.

### 17.10 Composite Confidence Display

If the interface displays one headline Confidence Score, it must identify whether it refers to Intrinsic Asset Value or Observed Market Price, expose the component measures, be calibrated against observable outcomes, avoid averaging incompatible measures without justification, and preserve critical warnings even when the composite appears moderate. Confidence is never a cosmetic badge: it must affect fair-value bands, classification, and interpretation.

## 18. Volatility Constitution

### 18.1 Required Volatility Objects

The system distinguishes five volatility objects. The object and horizon must always be named.

1. **Fantasy Output Volatility** — expected variability in fantasy scoring over the scoring horizon. Relevant to lineup decisions; never the canonical asset-volatility metric.
2. **Intrinsic Value Revision Volatility** — expected dispersion of future changes in Intrinsic Asset Value as new football evidence arrives. The primary candidate for the displayed headline metric.
3. **Market Price Volatility** — expected dispersion of future changes in Observed Market Price, reflecting sentiment, liquidity, source concentration, event reaction, seasonal cycles, and herd behavior.
4. **Event Sensitivity** — the magnitude and direction of value change conditional on specified events (starter injury, role promotion, trade, extension, suspension, rookie draft capital). Scenario-based, never inferred solely from historical price movement. Future event sensitivity matters even when recent price has been stable.
5. **Career-State Transition Risk** — probability and consequence of transitions among durable career or role states (starter to committee, active to injured reserve, developmental to starter, rostered to replacement-level).

### 18.2 Asymmetry

Volatility outputs must permit asymmetric upside and downside. A single standard deviation is insufficient when the distribution is skewed or event-driven.

### 18.3 Display Naming Rule

The interface must not label asset-value volatility simply as "Volatility." The headline label is **Asset Value Volatility**, and weekly scoring consistency is displayed separately.

## 19. Event Response Policy

### 19.1 General Reaction Rule

The magnitude and speed of any update depend on: event confirmation; source reliability; novelty; structural impact; expected duration; prior probability; affected horizons; reversibility; sample size; and player-specific sensitivity. Update size reflects information content, never dramatic salience.

### 19.2 Immediate Reaction

Immediate reaction is required for: confirmed season-ending injury; official trade; release; suspension; retirement; starting-role announcement from a credible source; inactive status near lock; major contract event affecting roster security; clear depth-chart change supported by actual deployment; confirmed return from major absence with role evidence.

For confirmed injuries, effects separate by horizon: Weekly — availability and workload; Rest of Season — recovery and reinjury; One Year — surgery, recurrence, role competition; Three Year and Dynasty — long-term impairment. For completed trades, the model evaluates integration delay, scheme fit, competition, team volume, contract incentives, vacated opportunity, and effects on displaced teammates; a new team is never assumed automatically better.

### 19.3 Discounted or Ignored Information

Information is ignored or heavily discounted when it is: not new; already fully priced (subject to P22 — this affects market and classification layers, never suppresses a genuine intrinsic state update); unsourced; promotional; non-specific; contradicted by stronger evidence; based on tiny samples with no role change; unrelated to future fantasy utility; duplicated across outlets from one original source; or generated by market movement itself.

Specific standing policies:

- **Single-game results:** react modestly to isolated good or bad games. Interrogate opportunity, role, health, matchup, and repeatability before moving value. React materially only when the game confirms a role change, high-value opportunity shift, or resolution of prior uncertainty.
- **Preseason and camp:** default skepticism. First-team usage, route participation, alignment, depth-chart movement, and corroborated multi-source practice observation are admissible; highlight clips, generic coach praise, and "best shape" narratives are not.
- **Rumors:** classified by source track record, directness, corroboration, specificity, plausibility, and time sensitivity. The intrinsic pipeline waits for stronger evidence; the market pipeline reflects observed reaction while flagging low-confidence movement.
- **Coaching changes:** staged response — immediate uncertainty increase plus known scheme tendencies; confirmation deferred to observed offseason usage, depth-chart decisions, and deployment. Reputation alone never creates a large intrinsic move.
- **Suspensions:** confirmed discipline updates availability immediately; rumored discipline increases uncertainty without pricing a full absence.

### 19.4 Reversal and Confirmation

Every event-driven update carries a reversal or confirmation policy: camp hype decays without regular-season confirmation; a temporary injury replacement loses value on the starter's return; a trade-rumor premium expires with the deadline; a role gain becomes more durable with repeated deployment.

## 20. Normalization and Display Governance

### 20.1 Display Purpose and Scale

The displayed value is a bounded 0–100 communication layer over the common latent valuation unit. It must never become the hidden economic model. The display scale is intuitive, nonlinear in economic meaning, stable across routine updates, capable of elite separation, anchored to format-specific replacement, and horizon-specific.

A purely linear interpretation is prohibited: a ten-point difference near replacement does not carry the same economic meaning as a ten-point difference among elite assets. Unbounded dollar or pick-equivalent scales may be offered as later conversion layers; they never define the canonical model.

The displayed value represents a normalized estimate of the asset's fantasy utility relative to the relevant player universe, replacement environment, positional scarcity, and selected horizon. It does not represent projected points, probability of success, percentile outcome, trade fairness, a guaranteed acquisition price, or a universal cross-format value.

### 20.2 Shared Mapping Requirement

For the same league format, horizon, asset type, and scale version, Intrinsic Asset Value and Observed Market Price use the same latent-to-display mapping. The pipelines are independent; the display transformation is shared. Independently fitted display transformations are prohibited (Section 5.4).

### 20.3 Stable Anchors

Normalization uses governed anchors defined in the latent unit under declared reference-league assumptions, not the current player distribution alone. Required anchor classes:

- effective zero / non-rosterable boundary;
- reference replacement zone;
- reference starter zone;
- premium starter / cornerstone zone;
- elite reference zone.

Interpretive display regions (e.g., 90–100 rare franchise assets; 80–89 premium cornerstones; 70–79 strong core starters; 60–69 material starters; 50–59 useful but replaceable; below 50 depth, speculative, contingent, or near-waiver assets; 0 outside the modeled universe) are conceptual anchors, not fitted thresholds and not equal-width economic tiers. The top of the scale is harder to enter and more economically separated. Replacement is not a universal "50"; the interface shows a format- and position-specific Replacement Line.

### 20.4 Movement Decomposition

The system distinguishes and stores three movement types:

1. **Underlying Value Movement** — change in latent value;
2. **Relative Position Movement** — change relative to the player universe;
3. **Normalization Movement** — change caused by a scale mapping or anchor revision.

A player whose latent value is unchanged must never be shown as having model-driven movement merely because the population or mapping changed.

### 20.5 Historical Records

Historical records store: raw model outputs; common latent values; displayed values; scale version; league schema version; horizon; player-universe definition; anchor definitions. Historical charts default to values as published at the time. Reconstructed history under a later model may be offered separately and must be labeled as reconstructed.

### 20.6 Scale-Version Changes

A scale-version change requires: a documented reason; a bridge mapping; old-versus-new distribution analysis; impact analysis on elite and replacement regions; confirmation that no classification changed solely through display mapping; user-facing disclosure for material changes.

### 20.7 Interpreting Score Differences

A displayed-score difference is interpreted through scale location, confidence overlap, volatility, tier boundaries, format, horizon, and liquidity. The interface must never imply that a four-point gap has a universal meaning, and must communicate overlap through intervals, tiers, or probability-based comparison.

## 21. Classification Constitution

### 21.1 Classification Space

Mispricing classification is determined in the common latent valuation unit. Displayed-score subtraction is prohibited as a classification basis.

### 21.2 Required Inputs

Classification uses: the Intrinsic Asset Value distribution; the Observed Market Price distribution; shared-data covariance if any; market liquidity; format and horizon match; event-processing status; data quality; model-version compatibility; expected transaction costs or spread where available.

### 21.3 Materiality

A valuation gap is material only when it exceeds a governed threshold relative to uncertainty and execution conditions. No fixed displayed-score difference defines materiality across scale regions. Tiny gaps never generate automatic labels.

### 21.4 Classification Strength

Strength reflects: the probability that the latent gap has the claimed sign; expected gap magnitude; Market-Price Confidence; Intrinsic-Estimate Confidence; liquidity; robustness under sensitivity analysis.

### 21.5 No Classification

No Classification is mandatory when any critical condition fails: incompatible league formats; incompatible horizons; stale or unprocessed material event; unresolved identity issue; insufficient market evidence; invalid data contract; excessive model disagreement; incompatible model or normalization versions; package-decomposition uncertainty beyond threshold; a critical missing input without validated fallback. Forcing a label is worse than admitting insufficient evidence.

### 21.6 Language Rule

The technical system may retain overvalued / fairly valued / undervalued internally. The interface pairs classifications with precise language — **Market Below Model Range**, **Market Inside Model Range**, **Market Above Model Range** — to avoid implying that the model has discovered objective truth. "Fairly valued" does not mean the point estimates are identical.

## 22. Explanation Constitution

### 22.1 Explanation Types

Explanations distinguish: model attribution; observed event; inferred state change; market reaction; causal evidence; unresolved hypothesis. Driver explanations are model attributions unless stronger causal evidence exists.

### 22.2 No False Causality

The system never states that a feature "caused" a change without a justified causal claim. Preferred language: contributed to; was associated with; changed the model estimate through; is consistent with; increased the probability of.

### 22.3 Movement Decomposition

Every material change separates: new football evidence; forecast revision; utility conversion effect; market-observation change; confidence change; normalization effect; model-version effect.

### 22.4 Explanation Stability

Explanation systems are tested for consistency across repeated runs, sensitivity to correlated features, agreement with actual model contribution, avoidance of contradictory reasons, correct timestamping, and faithful separation of model and market movement.

### 22.5 Honest Incompleteness

When no dominant explanation exists, the system states that the movement is distributed across signals or unresolved. Fabricated narrative unity is prohibited.

# PART VII — GOVERNANCE, VALIDATION, AND CONTRACTS

## 23. Signal Registry and Evidence Standards

### 23.1 Signal Registry

Every candidate signal is registered before it enters production, with:

| Field | Required judgment |
|---|---|
| Signal name | Clear, unique, non-marketing label |
| Definition | Exact meaning and unit |
| Position scope | QB, RB, WR, TE, or combination |
| Horizon scope | Weekly, ROS, one-year, three-year, dynasty |
| Conceptual family | Opportunity, efficiency, context, age, injury, contract, market, etc. |
| Data tier | Tier 1–4 per Section 8.1 |
| Predictive power | Expected and measured incremental out-of-sample value |
| Engineering cost | Acquisition, cleaning, storage, latency, licensing, legal risk, maintenance |
| Reliability | Accuracy, completeness, definitional stability, revision frequency, decision-time availability |
| Historical availability | Start date, missing seasons, coverage, granularity, revisions, definitional continuity |
| Update frequency | Matched to the rate at which the underlying state changes; faster is not automatically better |
| Noise risk | Sampling variance, measurement error, manipulation, regime sensitivity, survivorship and selection bias |
| MVP priority | Essential, useful, deferred, research-only, rejected |
| Evidence tag | Per Section 23.3 |
| Validation method | Predeclared per Section 25 |
| Correlation risk | Likely overlap with existing features |
| Interaction candidates | Signals whose interpretation changes this signal |
| Missingness policy | Per Section 8.4 |
| Explanation value | Whether it can be described clearly to users |
| Failure modes | Known ways the signal can mislead |
| Governance owner | Responsible model or data owner |

### 23.2 Assessment Scoring

Research review uses ordinal categories (Very Low, Low, Moderate, High, Very High, Unknown) with written rationale, never fake decimal precision. Predictive power means incremental out-of-sample value for a declared target, horizon, position, and baseline, with stability across eras and residual benefit after correlated signals are included. High univariate correlation does not establish incremental value.

### 23.3 Evidence Tags

Every material model decision carries exactly one tag:

1. **Evidence Supported** — direct, reproducible empirical testing relevant to the target population: documented data, defined target, out-of-sample validation, reproducible method, reported uncertainty, no obvious leakage. The strongest category; it means supported under the tested conditions, not permanently true.
2. **Strong Public Research** — credible published or publicly inspectable research with sound methodology. Suitable for priors and design direction; must be replicated internally before receiving high production influence.
3. **Industry Consensus** — widely accepted among credible practitioners but not established through accessible rigorous research. Provisional; consensus can be wrong, stale, or copied.
4. **Expert Informed** — structured, documented, attributed domain judgment where direct evidence is incomplete (injury interpretation, scheme fit, source credibility). Informs priors, scenarios, and labels; never masquerades as measured fact.
5. **Product Hypothesis** — a plausible design or modeling idea not yet empirically supported. Must be tested; never described publicly as proven.
6. **Temporary Placeholder** — a knowingly incomplete assumption used to unblock development. Requires labeling, an owner, replacement criteria, and a review date, and must be prevented from silently becoming permanent.

### 23.4 Research Decision Record

Every decision record includes: question; selected approach; alternatives considered; evidence tag; supporting evidence; known weaknesses; expected failure modes; affected positions and horizons; validation plan; review date; owner; implementation status.

## 24. Dependency and Leakage Rules

### 24.1 Forbidden Leakage Into Pure Intrinsic Models

The following are forbidden as direct inputs to Pure Intrinsic Asset Value (a separately named research output may study them under Section 12.8):

- current Observed Market Price;
- current trade-calculator score;
- current expert consensus rank;
- current social sentiment;
- current market momentum;
- current acquisition frequency used as a popularity proxy;
- the platform's own published market score;
- any information unavailable at valuation time.

### 24.2 Downstream Isolation

A downstream output never silently feeds an upstream object (Section 10). Normalization never uses the classification target. Explanation text never affects model inputs.

### 24.3 Permitted Football Information From Market Sources

A market source may reveal factual football information before structured feeds update. That information may enter the intrinsic pipeline only if: it is converted into a classified event; source reliability is recorded; the factual claim — not the market reaction — is modeled; novelty and confirmation are assessed; and the same rule applies whether or not the market moved.

### 24.4 Circularity Audit

Every production release tests whether: market inputs leak into intrinsic outputs; intrinsic outputs leak into market labels; normalization uses the classification target; explanation text affects model inputs; copied platform values re-enter as external consensus; backtests use later-known data.

## 25. Validation Constitution

### 25.1 Philosophy

No single metric validates the system. Projection, role estimation, career survival, market-price estimation, uncertainty quantification, ranking, mispricing identification, and explanation each require separate validation.

### 25.2 Estimand and Metric Hierarchy

Every model document defines its estimand before discussing features or weights, and declares a primary metric, secondary metrics, calibration metrics, stability constraints, business and UX guardrails, and a promotion rule when metrics disagree. A model may not be promoted for improving one convenient metric while damaging calibration, stability, or decision usefulness.

### 25.3 Historical Testing

Historical testing evaluates whether outputs would have been useful using only information available at the time. Targets include next-week points, rest-of-season points, next-season points, games played, role retention, top-N finishes, value change, market convergence, and trade-value preservation. Metrics include MAE, RMSE where tail penalties matter, rank correlation, proper probabilistic scoring rules, calibration, interval coverage, classification precision and recall, and decision utility.

### 25.4 Validation Splits

Random row-level cross-validation leaks player, team, season, and era information and is generally prohibited. Preferred: rolling-origin validation; season-based holdouts; blocked time-series splits; player-cohort holdouts; team or regime holdouts where relevant; nested validation for model selection. The split design mirrors actual deployment.

### 25.5 Locked Holdout Governance

The research program maintains protected evaluation data through one or more of: sealed future-season holdouts; rotating season holdouts; player-cohort holdouts; league-format holdouts; locked event windows; independent replication by a separate reviewer. The holdout process records how often it has been accessed.

### 25.6 Multiple-Testing Governance

When many candidate signals or interactions are tested, false discovery is controlled through predeclared hypotheses where practical, correction or shrinkage methods, replication requirements, locked holdouts, stability across eras and cohorts, and rejection of small fragile improvements.

### 25.7 Season Replay

Season replay is mandatory: freeze data at each historical timestamp; begin from preseason priors; process news, games, injuries, and market data sequentially; generate values at historical cadence; record every movement and explanation; compare against outcomes and observed market behavior; audit the largest misses and movements.

### 25.8 Mispricing Backtests

An asset labeled Market Below Model Range is evaluated against future production, future market-price appreciation, acquirability at the estimated price, risk-adjusted return, horizon-specific utility, and transaction and roster costs. A model can predict production correctly and still fail as a market model if the acquisition price was wrong or liquidity never existed.

### 25.9 Stress, Sensitivity, and Edge Cases

Stress tests simulate injury clusters, sudden role changes, rookie breakouts, veteran collapses, quarterback changes, trades, coaching changes, low-liquidity assets, missing feeds, delayed news, erroneous data, extreme touchdown variance, shortened seasons, and scoring-environment shifts. The goal is controlled behavior, not only accuracy.

Sensitivity analysis examines input-response curves, discontinuities, single-variable dominance, missing-data behavior, elite compression, fringe volatility, threshold behavior, and normalization dependence.

Required edge cases include: rookie with no NFL data; undrafted breakout; elite player returning from major injury; aging elite producer; backup quarterback in superflex; committee running back; blocking tight end with a touchdown spike; suspended player; unsigned veteran; position change; duplicate names; stale or conflicting depth charts; trade during a data refresh; zero recent market observations; retired player persisting in source data; scoring-rule extremes.

### 25.10 Calibration

All probabilistic outputs are calibration-tested: start probability, active probability, role retention, threshold finishes, games played, interval coverage, breakout and decline probabilities.

### 25.11 Baselines and Ablation

Every new model is compared against a naive prior, previous production, consensus projection, market price, expert consensus, the current production model, and a simple position baseline. Complexity that cannot beat sensible baselines is rejected. Ablation removes each major layer or signal family and measures accuracy, calibration, stability, explanation quality, subgroup performance, and cost; components that add complexity without measurable value do not survive.

### 25.12 Error Analysis

Largest errors are classified (role miss, injury miss, talent miss, environment miss, market-price miss, data error, news-timing miss, normalization issue, unavoidable randomness, regime failure) and recurring categories are tracked organizationally, not only aggregate metrics.

### 25.13 Release Stability

Weights and structure evolve through controlled releases: fixed retraining cadence; champion–challenger framework; out-of-sample promotion criteria; minimum practical improvement; stability thresholds; subgroup review; rollback capability; versioned outputs; shadow deployment; explanation regression tests; historical bridge analysis. Weekly refit-and-publish is prohibited unless the model is explicitly designed and stability-tested as a constrained online learner.

### 25.14 Drift Monitoring

Monitored: feature distributions; scoring environment; league strategy; position usage; residuals; calibration; market-source composition; missingness; provider definitions; relationship decay. Drift triggers investigation, never automatic unrestricted reweighting.

### 25.15 Joint and Correlated Outcomes

Research architecture supports joint distributions where materially relevant: quarterback and receiver; starter and backup running back; competing receivers; teammates sharing scoring opportunity; offensive environment shocks; concentrated roster exposure. Independence may be used as an MVP approximation only when documented and stress-tested.

### 25.16 Publication Effects

Validation distinguishes predictive accuracy before publication, market movement after publication, performance caused by market adoption, and genuine forecasting improvement (Section 9.7).

### 25.17 Archetype Consistency

The system tests whether it systematically mis-values rookies, older players, mobile quarterbacks, receiving and committee backs, slot receivers, deep threats, blocking tight ends, low-draft-capital breakouts, injured players, and players on weak offenses. Consistency here means analytical error control across football archetypes.

### 25.18 User-Outcome Validation

The product must eventually test whether users correctly understand and use confidence, volatility, horizon differences, fair-value ranges, No Classification, and roster-specific adjustments. A statistically good model with misleading UX is not a successful product.

## 26. Formula-Design Entry Gates

No QB, RB, WR, TE, rookie, pick, package, confidence, volatility, momentum, Observed Market Price, Intrinsic Asset Value, or decision formula may be approved until every gate is satisfied. A model failing any critical gate remains research-only.

- **Gate 1 — Terminology:** all outputs use the canonical ontology of Section 4.
- **Gate 2 — Estimand:** the mathematical target is explicit.
- **Gate 3 — Unit:** the native unit and common latent mapping are defined.
- **Gate 4 — Dependencies:** allowed and forbidden inputs are listed.
- **Gate 5 — Context:** position, horizon, league format, and asset type are declared.
- **Gate 6 — Data:** every production signal has a valid data contract and readiness tier.
- **Gate 7 — Uncertainty:** the relevant uncertainty and confidence objects are specified.
- **Gate 8 — Validation:** baselines, metrics, holdouts, calibration, sensitivity, and failure tests are defined.
- **Gate 9 — Historical reproduction:** point-in-time data and version requirements are satisfied.
- **Gate 10 — Explanation:** an auditable attribution path exists and false causal claims are avoided.
- **Gate 11 — Leakage:** the circularity audit passes.
- **Gate 12 — Degradation:** missing, stale, conflicting, or unavailable data have defined fallback or rejection behavior.

## 27. Output Contract

Every production valuation must be capable of returning:

**Identification and context:** canonical asset identity; asset type; position where applicable; league schema version; reference format; user league format where applicable; time horizon; valuation timestamp; information cutoff timestamp.

**Forecast and utility:** Expected Fantasy Output distribution; principal state probabilities; Fundamental Player Utility; Intrinsic Asset Value in latent units with interval; displayed Intrinsic Value; replacement baseline; scarcity contribution; terminal assumptions where applicable.

**Market:** Observed Market Price in latent units with interval; displayed Market Price; source-class composition; effective observation count; source dispersion; liquidity estimate; freshness; estimated execution range where available.

**Uncertainty and volatility:** State Uncertainty; Forecast Uncertainty; Intrinsic-Estimate Confidence; Market-Price Confidence; Data-Quality Confidence; Explanation Confidence; Fantasy Output Volatility; Intrinsic Value Revision Volatility; Market Price Volatility; upside and downside Event Sensitivity.

**Classification and decision:** latent valuation gap; probability the gap is positive or negative; classification; classification strength; No Classification reason where applicable; user-specific Decision Value where enabled; roster adjustment and rationale; package effects where applicable.

**Explanation and governance:** top positive and negative model attributions; conflicting signals; material events; unresolved hypotheses; next evidence that could change the estimate; model, feature, data, normalization, and explanation versions; reconstruction status; missing-data warnings; rights or source restrictions where relevant.

## 28. Constitutional Test Cases

Before any consolidated architecture is approved, it must produce coherent, auditable, and non-circular outputs for at least:

1. A rookie with high upside, low state confidence, and high Market Price.
2. A veteran with strong weekly utility, weak three-year utility, and liquid dynasty demand.
3. A backup running back with low current utility but high event sensitivity.
4. A receiver with strong target share on low route participation.
5. A player whose latent value is unchanged while the surrounding player pool changes.
6. A player whose Market Price rises on sentiment without football evidence.
7. A player whose Intrinsic Asset Value rises before the market reacts.
8. A player whose market and intrinsic estimates share a factual news source but not a price signal.
9. A multi-player trade in which arithmetic score addition produces an obviously bad equivalence.
10. A future rookie pick with uncertain slot and class quality.
11. A player with sparse trade observations but abundant draft behavior.
12. A player with high market liquidity but uncertain football state.
13. A player with high intrinsic confidence but high future event volatility.
14. A user for whom a globally attractive asset has low marginal roster utility.
15. A model-version change that alters displayed values without changing raw latent utility.
16. A copied consensus source appearing through several downstream products.
17. A completed trade reflecting a contender premium rather than general market value.
18. A data outage removing route participation from one provider.
19. A breaking injury report that is credible but medically incomplete.
20. A published platform valuation later copied into an external market source.

## 29. Freeze and Approval Standard

This foundation is frozen — and formula design may begin — only when an independent review confirms all of the following:

1. No core term has multiple incompatible meanings.
2. Intrinsic and market pipelines are independent where required.
3. Both values share a common latent comparison unit.
4. Display normalization cannot manufacture mispricing.
5. Historical movements distinguish model, market, and normalization effects.
6. Confidence objects are separate and operationally defined.
7. Volatility objects are separate and operationally defined.
8. Dynasty utility and dynasty market demand are not conflated.
9. Picks and packages are first-class assets.
10. Roster personalization is downstream and transparent.
11. Market-source lineage and dependence are governed.
12. Data readiness and legal use are release gates.
13. Every future formula has an estimand, unit, dependency contract, uncertainty object, and validation plan.
14. No critical output depends on unavailable aspirational data.
15. Two independent engineering teams would interpret the architecture materially the same way.

If any critical condition fails, the foundation remains unapproved.

## 30. Authoritative Glossary

Each term below has exactly one canonical meaning across all platform documents. Where a definition elsewhere in this document is more detailed, that section controls; no other document may redefine these terms.

**Asset** — A player, prospect, pick, contract right, or other supported object that can be owned or exchanged in a fantasy league.

**Asset Value Volatility** — The user-facing label for Intrinsic Value Revision Volatility. Never displayed as bare "Volatility."

**Classification** — The latent-space determination of whether Observed Market Price sits below, inside, or above the model's fair-value range, or that no determination is possible.

**Common Latent Valuation Unit** — The shared pre-display unit in which Intrinsic Asset Value and Observed Market Price are estimated and compared (Section 5).

**Confidence** — A qualified assessment of evidentiary and model support for a specified estimate. The object must always be named (Section 17).

**Data-Quality Confidence** — Confidence that required inputs are accurate, complete, timely, correctly mapped, and legally usable.

**Decision Value** — See User-Specific Decision Value.

**Display Value** — A normalized user-facing representation of a latent valuation. Never the canonical economic object.

**Dynasty Decision Value** — Roster-specific marginal utility for a particular dynasty manager (Section 14.5).

**Dynasty Fundamental Utility** — The discounted distribution of future fantasy utility under ongoing ownership, excluding current market preference and acquisition cost (Section 14.2).

**Dynasty Intrinsic Asset Value** — Dynasty Fundamental Utility converted into the common latent valuation unit with only permitted non-price characteristics (Section 14.3).

**Dynasty Market Price** — Current estimated exchange cost in the declared dynasty format (Section 14.4).

**Estimand** — The explicit mathematical object a model estimates. "Predict value" is not an estimand.

**Event Sensitivity** — Conditional value change under a specified future event (Section 18.1).

**Executable Trade Estimate** — Estimated range and probability of completing a specific transaction (Section 4.6).

**Expected Fantasy Output** — A probability distribution over future fantasy scoring outcomes for a declared player, scoring system, and horizon (Section 4.1).

**Explanation Confidence** — Confidence that a displayed explanation identifies the principal model-attributed contributors to an output or movement. Never a causal claim.

**Fantasy Output Volatility** — Expected variability in fantasy scoring over the scoring horizon. Not the canonical asset-volatility metric.

**Forecast Uncertainty** — Dispersion in future outcomes after conditioning on the current information state.

**Fundamental Player Utility** — Football-and-league utility independent of current acquisition cost and current market preference (Section 4.2).

**Intrinsic Asset Value** — Fundamental Player Utility translated into the common latent valuation unit, plus only explicitly permitted non-price asset characteristics (Section 4.3).

**Intrinsic-Estimate Confidence** — The degree of empirical support for the estimated Intrinsic Asset Value and its interval coverage.

**Intrinsic Value Revision Volatility** — Expected dispersion of future changes in Intrinsic Asset Value as new football evidence arrives.

**Liquidity** — The degree to which an asset can be exchanged near the estimated market price within a relevant period and without substantial concession.

**Market Package Price** — Observed acquisition cost for a package or its inferred components (Section 15.5).

**Market Price** — See Observed Market Price.

**Market-Price Confidence** — The degree to which the estimated Market Price is supported by recent, independent, relevant, and sufficiently liquid observations.

**Market Price Volatility** — Expected dispersion of future changes in Observed Market Price.

**Market Range** — Uncertainty interval around estimated consensus acquisition cost. Not automatically the executable bid–ask range.

**Momentum** — Direction, speed, breadth, and persistence of change in a named value object over a specified window.

**No Classification** — The mandatory output state when any critical classification condition fails (Section 21.5).

**Normalization** — The governed transformation from common latent value into a user-facing display scale.

**Observed Market Price** — The posterior estimate of current exchange cost derived solely from market observations and their reliability (Section 4.4).

**Pure Intrinsic Asset Value** — The production Intrinsic Asset Value that excludes current fantasy-market price and preference signals; the mandatory input to mispricing classification.

**Reference Package Value** — Package value under the canonical league and neutral roster assumptions (Section 15.5).

**Replacement** — The expected accessible alternative under a declared league environment, roster rule, asset pool, and horizon.

**Risk** — A qualified possibility of adverse or favorable outcomes. Technical documents must name the relevant risk object.

**Scarcity** — Marginal utility created by the distribution of usable alternatives relative to lineup and roster requirements.

**State Uncertainty** — Uncertainty about the player's current football state.

**User Decision Package Value** — Package value for the user's actual roster and strategy (Section 15.5).

**User-Specific Decision Value** — Roster- and user-specific marginal utility after applying constraints and preferences (Section 4.5).

**Volatility** — Expected dispersion of changes or outcomes in a named object. The object and horizon must always be stated (Section 18).

## 31. Final Constitutional Rule

The platform must never confuse:

- what a player is expected to produce;
- what that production is worth under declared fantasy rules;
- what the market currently charges;
- what the asset is worth to a particular roster;
- what transaction is likely to execute;
- how certain the platform is about any of those claims.

Every future model exists to estimate one of those objects. No future model is permitted to blur them for convenience.

The final standard for the finished product is a demanding question:

> Would a skeptical, technically literate fantasy player be able to understand what the model believes, why it believes it, how uncertain it is, what could change it, and whether the current market price offers a genuine decision advantage?

If the answer is no, the platform is not finished.
